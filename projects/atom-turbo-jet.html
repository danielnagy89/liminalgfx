Atom Turbo Jet

Working in the indie sphere I got too used to cutting corners. I wanted to check where current processes were compared to the industry standards, so I enrolled into a short mentorship with AMC Studio.

When teams are small and milestones come tumbling, you get in a mindset of getting things done at heavy cost. Corners get cut, errors get swept under the rug, whatever it takes to get to the finish line. I felt like I never did a project where I emphasized quality to the detriment of speed, so this here would be my exercise.

I wrote an application detailing who I am and what I was looking for, and within a few days I've received the confirmation email. I had a short call with my mentor and the facilitator, and it was off to the races.

What to do?

Usually for this sort of short mentorship, props are favored since they're easy to manage and there's a good concentration of detail to tackle.

On the other hand, I already had a decade of experience so I wouldn't really get bogged down in the basics. To me it was an issue of assessment- revealing blindspots, undoing bad habits and fine-tuning. I'd need a challenge to keep it interesting too.

Decisions decisions

Props weren't doing it for me. I wanted something bigger, but I was well aware I'd be doing this in my spare time, and ambitious as I usually am, I was wary of my tendency to fill my plate to burnout. My idea of toning it down went as follows:

"What if instead of a vehicle I did a... smaller vehicle?"

Presto, problem solved.

Accuracy Process

For this project I've tried to use an obscure method to replicating a 3D model accurately from a set of images. I've used the discontinued Autodesk Image Modeler (no longer on the market) to place a cluster of Locators (points with same location). Using the reference images and shifting the Locators basically puts markers.
