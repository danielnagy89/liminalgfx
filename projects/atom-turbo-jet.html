Working in the indie sphere I got too used to cutting corners. I wanted an industry-standard refresher, so I enrolled into a short mentorship with AMC Studio.

When teams are small and milestones come tumbling, you get in a mindset of getting things done at all cost. Corners get cut, errors get swept under the rug, whatever it takes to get to the finish line. I felt like I never did a project where I emphasized end-product quality to the detriment of speed, so this here would be my exercise.

I wrote an application detailing who I am and what I was looking for, and within a few days I've received the confirmation email. I had a short call with my mentor and the facilitator, and it was off to the races.

What to do?

Usually for these sort of short mentorships favor props since they're easy to manage and there's a good concentration of detail to tackle. On the other hand, I already had a decade of experience so I wouldn't really get bogged down in the basics. To me it was an issue of assessment- revealing blindspots, undoing bad habits and fine-tuning. I'd need a challenge to keep it interesting too.

Decisions decisions

Props weren't doing it for me. I wanted something bigger, but I was well aware I'd be doing this in my spare time and wary of my tendency to fill my plate to burnout. My idea of toning it down went as follows:

"What if instead of a vehicle I did a... smaller vehicle?"

Presto, problem solved.

Reference

I'm fascinated from 'Atomic Age' aesthetics, the corny space-race metal toys in particular really caught my eye. The base inspiration was a classic-looking bumpercar from the 1950s; luckily I found a website that had one up for sale with a very comprehensive photo set, showing me all angles.

Accuracy Process

For this project I've tried to use an obscure method to replicating a 3D model accurately from a set of images. I've used the discontinued Autodesk Image Modeler (no longer on the market) to place a cluster of Locators (points with same location). How it works is, AIM loads the images and assigns a camera with specific settings to each image. The locators are shared between the cameras/ images, so adjusting them by what you see in each image will give it the spatial location relative to the cameras.

As I was about to find, it's a painstaking process and only worth it if you need extreme accuracy. The precise number of people who cared whether my bumpercar was life-accurate: ∅ (barred zero).

I've tried replicating this process with Perspective Plotter, an addon for Blender which does camera matching, but it only references one image. While working on this, I reached out to Mark Kingsnorth (the author of the plugin) to ask him about camera matching. He was interested in making a successor to AIM, since there's demand.

Review

  • ✔️ Remember AAA asset pipeline top-to-bottom
  • ✔️ Deepened experience with Substance Painter, came up with some generator tricks, Passthrough layers and Anchor Points.
  • ✔️ Tried Camera Matching → Control Points → Curves → Curves to Mesh workflow. Good option, but cumbersome at the moment. Suggested to Mike Kingsnorth to create a replacement for Autodesk Image Modeller, went nowhere.
  • ✔️ Scouting-ahead workflow, putting the asset in the next step to see what problems turn up.
  • ✔️ Tried RizomUV and determined it's superfluous for this use. The roundtripping isn't worth it when you have Blender/ ZenUV/ UVPackmaster stack. Being able to edit the geometry while unwrapping is essential.
  • ✔️ Learned Marmoset Toolbag bake and presentation workflow.
  • 🟡 Stubbornly packed everything on one texture (indie atlasing inertia). I could have benefited from a couple of UDIMs.
